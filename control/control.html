Spatial Control

State Representation

The three-dimensional state vector, \(X\), entirely describes the drone's state at a point in time.

\( X = [x, y, z, \phi, \theta, \psi, \dot{x}, \dot{y}, \dot{z}, \dot{\hat\phi}, \dot{\hat\theta}, \dot{\hat\psi}] \)

where

  • \(x, y, z\) denote the drone's positional coordinates in space,
  • \(\dot{x}, \dot{y}, \dot{z}\) represent linear velocities along the x, y, and z axes,
  • \(\phi, \theta, \psi\) stand for rotational angles around the x, y, and z axes,
  • \(\dot{\hat\phi}, \dot{\hat\theta}, \dot{\hat\psi}\) indicate rotational velocities within the drone's frame of reference.

A counterclockwise rotation of any vector around the \(x\), \(y\) or \(z\)-axis by some angle \(\alpha\) is done through multiplying it from the right to \(R_x(\alpha)\), \(R_y(\alpha)\) or \(R_z(\alpha)\).

\[ \begin{array}{cc} R_x(\alpha) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \cos(\alpha) & -\sin(\alpha) \\ 0 & \sin(\alpha) & \cos(\alpha) \\ \end{bmatrix} & R_y(\alpha) = \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\alpha) & 0 & \sin(\alpha) \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ -\sin(\alpha) & 0 & \cos(\alpha) \\ \end{bmatrix} \end{array} \] \[ R_z(\alpha) = \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\alpha) & -\sin(\alpha) & 0 \\ \sin(\alpha) & \cos(\alpha) & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Control Formulation

The control of co-axial rotored drones is a complex task that integrates various advanced control theories and algorithms. These include the Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) framework, state-space representation, non-linear control theories, and Newton's laws of motion. The PID controllers, forming the foundational layer of the control strategy, dynamically adjust rotor speeds to maintain stability and respond to control inputs, while also being complemented by advanced Model Predictive Control (MPC) algorithms.

\( \mathbf{T} = \mathbf{K}_p \cdot \mathbf{e} + \mathbf{K}_i \int \mathbf{e} \, dt + \mathbf{K}_d \frac{d\mathbf{e}}{dt} \)

Where \( \mathbf{T} \) is the thrust vector, \( \mathbf{K}_p, \mathbf{K}_i, \mathbf{K}_d \) are the PID coefficients, \( \mathbf{e} \) is the error vector, and \( u(t) \) is the control signal.

Advanced scenarios may involve adaptive control techniques like Model Predictive Control (MPC), or machine learning algorithms. These approaches optimize control inputs by predicting future states and adjusting for uncertainties and nonlinearities in real-time.

\[ \dot{X} = AX + BU \] \[ Y = CX + DU \]

Here, \(X\) is the state vector, \(U\) is the control input vector, \(Y\) is the output vector, and \(A\), \(B\), \(C\), and \(D\) are the system matrices defining the dynamics.

Rotor Speed Update

Rotor speed is a critical factor in maintaining the stability of a co-axial rotored drone. The rotor speed update is a continuous process and forms the core of the drone's flight control mechanism, ensuring efficient, responsive, and stable maneuvering in three-dimensional space. The thrust generated by each rotor is described by the following equation:

\( \omega_i = \sqrt{\frac{T_i}{k_f}} \)

Here, \( \omega_i \) represents the angular velocity of the ith rotor, \( T_i \) is the thrust produced by the ith rotor, and \( k_f \) is the thrust coefficient.

Additionally, the torque generated by each rotor, which contributes to the drone's yaw control, can be expressed as \( τ_i = k_m ω_i^2 \), where \(τ_i\) is the torque generated by the ith rotor, and \(k_m\) is the drag coefficient.
