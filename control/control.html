Spatial Control

State Representation

The three-dimensional state vector, \(X\), entirely describes the drone's state at a point in time.

\( X = [x, y, z, \phi, \theta, \psi, \dot{x}, \dot{y}, \dot{z}, \dot{\hat\phi}, \dot{\hat\theta}, \dot{\hat\psi}] \)

where

  • \(x, y, z\) denote the drone's positional coordinates in space,
  • \(\dot{x}, \dot{y}, \dot{z}\) represent linear velocities along the x, y, and z axes,
  • \(\phi, \theta, \psi\) stand for rotational angles around the x, y, and z axes,
  • \(\dot{\hat\phi}, \dot{\hat\theta}, \dot{\hat\psi}\) indicate rotational velocities within the drone's frame of reference.

A counterclockwise rotation of any vector around the \(x\), \(y\) or \(z\)-axis by some angle \(\alpha\) is done through multiplying it from the right to \(R_x(\alpha)\), \(R_y(\alpha)\) or \(R_z(\alpha)\).

\[ \begin{array}{cc} R_x(\alpha) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \cos(\alpha) & -\sin(\alpha) \\ 0 & \sin(\alpha) & \cos(\alpha) \\ \end{bmatrix} & R_y(\alpha) = \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\alpha) & 0 & \sin(\alpha) \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ -\sin(\alpha) & 0 & \cos(\alpha) \\ \end{bmatrix} \end{array} \] \[ R_z(\alpha) = \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\alpha) & -\sin(\alpha) & 0 \\ \sin(\alpha) & \cos(\alpha) & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Comprehensive Control Formulation and Rotor Speed Update for Co-Axial Rotored Drones

The control system of co-axial rotored drones integrates principles from aerodynamics, mechanics, control theory, and advanced mathematics. It addresses nonlinear dynamics, achieving precise stability, navigation efficiency, and responsiveness. This is achieved through sophisticated algorithms, including state-space representation, PID (Proportional, Integral, Derivative) controllers, and, in advanced scenarios, adaptive techniques like Model Predictive Control (MPC).

Control Formulation Approaches

The primary control mechanism, the PID controller, continuously adjusts rotor speeds to reduce discrepancies between the drone's current state and the desired state. This is mathematically expressed as:

\( u(t) = K_p e(t) + K_i \int_{0}^{t} e(\tau) d\tau + K_d \frac{d}{dt} e(t) \)
where \( u(t) \) is the control input, \( e(t) \) is the error signal, and \( K_p, K_i, K_d \) are the controller gains.

In addition to PID control, state-space models, using equations like \( \dot{X} = AX + BU \) and \( Y = CX + DU \), describe the system dynamics and integrate MPC for predictive control over a future time horizon. The MPC optimization problem is formulated to minimize discrepancies over a set time frame.

The control laws are underpinned by Newton's laws of motion, particularly focusing on linear and angular momentum principles. Advanced control formulations involve calculating the appropriate rotor speeds based on desired movement, current state of the drone, and external factors like inertia and external forces.

Rotor Speed Update Mechanism

Rotor speed updates, crucial for achieving desired thrust, torque, and maneuverability, are governed by the dynamics of the drone's motion. The thrust and torque generated by each rotor can be described by equations like \( F_i = k_f \omega_i^2 \) and \( \tau_i = k_m \omega_i^2 \), where \( F_i \) and \( \tau_i \) are thrust and torque, respectively, \( \omega_i \) is the rotor speed, and \( k_f, k_m \) are constants.

The control system continually adjusts rotor speeds in real-time, balancing thrust generation, torque, and momentum control. Electronic Speed Controllers (ESCs) serve as the interface between control algorithms and motors, modulating power based on control inputs and sensor feedback.

Advanced algorithms compensate for asymmetries in rotor performance and external disturbances like wind, ensuring efficient, responsive, and stable maneuvering. The control loop architecture integrates sensor feedback for continual adjustment, maintaining equilibrium and efficient power usage under various conditions.
