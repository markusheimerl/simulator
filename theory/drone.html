A formalization of co-axial rotored aerodynamics

Markus Heimerl
Ratisbon Robotics
Regensburg, Germany
markusheimerl@ratisbonrobotics.com

Abstract—This work presents a formalization of co-axial dual and quad rotor aerodynamic principles, integral branches of fluid dynamics. This effort aims to establish a solid theoretical foundation for aeronautics and related disciplines.

Keywords—drone dynamics, formalization, 2D and 3D frameworks, aerodynamic principles, theoretical modeling, fluid dynamics, thrust dynamics, rotational mechanics, mathematical representation.

Introduction

The objective of this work is to formalize the principles of co-axial dual and quad rotor aerodynamics, a specialized area within fluid dynamics. Co-axial rotors refer to systems where multiple rotors are aligned along the same rotational axis, commonly facing in the same direction. This formalization provides a foundational basis for practical applications in aeronautics, specifically in the design and operation of airborne vehicles. In this context, "drone" refers to both dual and quad rotor systems, where dual rotor implies a system with two rotating propellers and quad rotor denotes a system with four.

Two Dimensions

State Representation

A simulation is considered comprehensive if it can predict the drone's state \(X\) for subsequent discrete time intervals.

\( X = [x, y, \phi, \dot{x}, \dot{y}, \dot{\phi}] \)

Here, \(X\) encapsulates the drone's coordinates (\(x, y\)), rotational angle (\(\phi\)) about the x-axis, and their corresponding velocities (\(\dot{x}, \dot{y}, \dot{\phi}\)). The thrust forces (\(F_{1}\) and \(F_{2}\)) from the rotors, combined with environmental factors, result in the alteration of the state vector \(X\). The thrusts \(F_{1}\) and \(F_{2}\) are angled relative to the y-axis by \(\phi\).

Dynamics Formulation

The dynamics of the drone are characterized by how its state \(X\) evolves under the influence of forces \(F_{1}\) and \(F_{2}\). These forces, as a function of rotor velocities (\(\omega_{1}, \omega_{2}\)), follow a relationship with thrust, denoted by \(k_{f}\), that is derived from the principles of fluid dynamics and aerodynamics. These disciplines, building upon Newton's Laws of Motion 1, specifically address the interaction between the rotors and the surrounding air. The force generated by the rotors is proportional to the square of their velocity.

\( F_{i} = k_{f} \omega_{i}^2 \)

The thrust components \(F_{x}\) and \(F_{y}\), acting along the x-axis and y-axis respectively, are determined by trigonometric relationships, based on principles established in Euclid's work 4, derived from the total thrust force (\(F_{total} = F_1 + F_2\)) and the orientation angle (\(\phi\)).

\[ F_{y} = \cos(\phi)F_{total} \qquad F_{x} = \sin(\phi)F_{total} \]

Changes in the drone's position (\(x, y, \phi\)) are derived from the integration of velocities (\(\dot{x}, \dot{y}, \dot{\phi}\)), which in turn stem from the integration of accelerations (\(\ddot{x}, \ddot{y}, \ddot{\phi}\)). These accelerations are calculated using Newton's second law (\(F = ma\)) 1 and the gravitational force (\(F_{g} = mg\)), with \(g\) being earth's gravitational constant \( 9.81 \frac{m}{s^2} \).

\[ \ddot{y} = \frac{(F_{y} - F_{g})}{m} = \frac{F_{y}}{m} - g \qquad \ddot{x} = \frac{F_{x}}{m} \]

We employ two torque formulas (\(M_{x}\)), each with distinct historical and physical foundations. The first, \(M_{x} = F_{\phi} L\), originates from Archimedes' concept of leverage 3, where \(F_{\phi} = F_{2} - F_{1}\) represents the force from the drone's rotors and \(L\) is the lever arm - the distance from the center of mass to the motors. This equation captures the rotational effect of force. The second, \(M_{x} = I_{x} \ddot{\phi}\), is grounded in Newton's laws of motion 1, linking torque (\(M_{x}\)) with the drone's moment of inertia (\(I_{x}\)) and angular acceleration (\(\ddot{\phi}\)). Together, these formulas allow us to determine the angular acceleration of the drone.

\[ M_{x} = F_{\phi}L \\ I_{x} \ddot{\phi} = (F_{2} - F_{1})L \\ \ddot{\phi} = \frac{(F_{2} - F_{1}) L}{I_{x}} \]

The state of the drone at time \( t + \Delta t \) is determined by the sum of its current state \( X_t \) and the integral of the state's time derivative \( \dot{X} \) over the interval \( \Delta t \). This integral represents a precise calculation of the state change. However, for computational simplicity, we often use an approximation method developed by Leonhard Euler 2. His method approximates the future state by assuming a constant rate of change (\( \dot{X}_t \)) over a small interval (\( \Delta t \approx 0.01\)), simplifying the integral to a product of the rate of change and the time interval.

\[ X_{t + \Delta t} = X_{t} + \int_{t}^{t+\Delta t} \dot{X}_{\tau} d\tau \approx X_t + \Delta t\dot{X}_t \]

To thoroughly describe the time evolution of the drone's state, we substitude the explicit expressions for the accelerations \(\ddot{x}\), \(\ddot{y}\), and \(\ddot{\phi}\) into the equation for \( X_{t + \Delta t} \). These accelerations are directly derived from the drone's dynamics, which include the influence of forces and torques resulting from the rotor thrusts and earth's gravity.

\( X_{t + \Delta t} \approx X_t + \Delta t \dot{X}_t= \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ \phi \\ \dot{x} \\ \dot{y} \\ \dot{\phi} \end{bmatrix}_t + \Delta t \begin{bmatrix} \dot{x} \\ \dot{y} \\ \dot{\phi} \\ \ddot{x} \\ \ddot{y} \\ \ddot{\phi} \end{bmatrix}_t = \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ \phi \\ \dot{x} \\ \dot{y} \\ \dot{\phi} \end{bmatrix}_t + \begin{bmatrix} \Delta t\dot{x} \\ \Delta t\dot{y} \\ \Delta t\dot{\phi} \\ \frac{\Delta t\sin(\phi)(F_1 + F_2)}{m} \\ \Delta t(g - \frac{\cos(\phi)(F_1 + F_2)}{m}) \\ \frac{\Delta tL(F_{2} - F_{1})}{I_{x}} \end{bmatrix}_t \)

Three Dimensions

State Representation

The three-dimensional state vector, \(X\), extends the two-dimensional model by integrating additional spatial and rotational parameters. It is crucial for representing the drone's comprehensive state in three-dimensional space.

\( X = [x, y, z, \phi, \theta, \psi, \dot{x}, \dot{y}, \dot{z}, p, q, r] \)

The elements of state vector \(X\) include:

  • \(x, y, z\): The drone's positional coordinates in 3D space,
  • \(\dot{x}, \dot{y}, \dot{z}\): Linear velocities along the x, y, and z axes,
  • \(\phi, \theta, \psi\): Euler rotational angles around the x, y, and z axes, respectively,
  • \(p, q, r\): Angular velocities related to the above axes, within the drone's frame of reference.

It is imperative to differentiate between the "world coordinate system", an external fixed reference frame, and the "body coordinate system", which is intrinsic to the drone and changes with its movements and orientation.

Dynamics Formulation

Equation (2) still applies here as before. Analogous to Equation 10, in the three-dimensional case, we also require the derivative \(\dot{X}\) of the state vector to move the state forward in time. Equations (3) still apply but now, analogous to \(\ddot{y}\), we add the formula for \(\ddot{x}\). In summary, still derived from Newton's \(F = ma\), we have

\[ \ddot{z} = \frac{F_{g} - F_{z}}{m} = g - F_{z} \qquad \ddot{y} = \frac{F_{y}}{m} \qquad \ddot{x} = \frac{F_{x}}{m} \]

Different from the two-dimensional case, however, are the formulas for \(F_{x}\), \(F_{y}\), and \(F_{z}\). Here a rotation matrix is defined, which transforms any vector from the body coordinate system into a vector in the world coordinate system through multiplication. Essentially, the matrix is still based on triangle trigonometry. The matrix \(R_{Body}^{World}\) is

\begin{align*} R_{Body}^{World} &= \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \cos(\phi) & -\sin(\phi) \\ 0 & \sin(\phi) & \cos(\phi) \\ \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} \cos(\theta) & 0 & \sin(\theta) \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ -\sin(\theta) & 0 & \cos(\theta) \\ \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} \cos(\psi) & -\sin(\psi) & 0 \\ \sin(\psi) & \cos(\psi) & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ \end{array} \right] \\ &= \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} c(\psi)c(\theta) & c(\psi)s(\theta)s(\phi) - s(\psi)c(\phi) & s(\psi)s(\phi) + c(\psi)s(\theta)c(\phi) \\ s(\psi)c(\theta) & c(\psi)c(\phi) + s(\psi)s(\theta)s(\phi) & s(\psi)s(\theta)c(\phi) - c(\psi)s(\phi) \\ -s(\theta) & c(\theta)s(\phi) & c(\theta)c(\phi) \end{array} \right] \end{align*}

So, from Equations (22) and (23), we derive the formulas for axis accelerations in the world coordinate system as

\[ \begin{bmatrix} \ddot{x} \\ \ddot{y} \\ \ddot{z} \end{bmatrix} = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 0 \\ g \end{array} \right] + \frac{1}{m}\: R_{Body}^{World} \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 0 \\ -F_{total} \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{c} R_{13}\frac{1}{m}(-F_{total}) \\ R_{23}\frac{1}{m}(-F_{total}) \\ g + R_{33}\frac{1}{m}(-F_{total}) \end{array} \right] \]

Here it is also shown that the propellers generate thrust in the direction \(-kz\). On Earth, \(g = 9.81 \frac{m}{s^2}\). And the total force now results from the sum of all four rotors, so that

\( F_{total} = \sum_{1}^{i} F_{i} = F_{1} + F_{2} + F_{3} + F_{4} \)

The accelerations around the body axes \(\dot{p}\), \(\dot{q}\), and \(\dot{r}\) are calculated by the Euler's equation of motion, which is

\[ M = I\dot{\omega} + \omega \times (I\omega) = \begin{bmatrix} M_{x} \\ M_{y} \\ M_{z} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} I_{x} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & I_{y} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & I_{z} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \dot{p} \\ \dot{q} \\ \dot{r} \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} p \\ q \\ r \end{bmatrix} \times \left( \begin{bmatrix} I_{x} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & I_{y} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & I_{z} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} p \\ q \\ r \end{bmatrix} \right) \]

The moments of inertia \(I_{x}\), \(I_{y}\), and \(I_{z}\) are determined by experiments, as explained in Chapter Simulation Parameter Determination. By rearranging, we then get the accelerations as

\[ \begin{bmatrix} \dot{p} \\ \dot{q} \\ \dot{r} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{M_{x} - rq(I_{x} - I_{y})}{I_{x}} \\ \frac{M_{y} - rp(I_{x} - I_{z})}{I_{y}} \\ \frac{M_{z} - pq(I_{y} - I_{x})}{I_{z}} \end{bmatrix} \]

As remaining necessary quantities to establish \(\dot{X}\), which is the goal in dynamics, we have the velocities about the world coordinate system axes \(\dot{\phi}\), \(\dot{\theta}\), and \(\dot{\psi}\). These are obtained, like the linear accelerations in Equation \ref{eq:24} with the help of a rotation matrix specifically for Euler angle velocities.

\[ \begin{bmatrix} \dot{\phi} \\ \dot{\theta} \\ \dot{\psi} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & \sin(\phi)\tan(\theta) & \cos(\phi)\tan(\theta) \\ 0 & \cos(\phi) & -\sin(\phi) \\ 0 & \sin(\phi)/\cos(\theta) & \cos(\phi)/\cos(\theta) \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} p \\ q \\ r \end{bmatrix} \]

Thus, the state of the quadrotor at time \(t\) can be determined by the sum of the state \(X(t)\) and the integral of the state's derivative \(\dot{X}\).

\[ X(t + \Delta t) = X(t) + \int_{t}^{t+\Delta t}\dot{X}(\tau) d\tau \]
